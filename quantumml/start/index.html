2.2 Wave-particle

In quantum mechanics, wave-particle duality refers to the wave and particle property displayed by microscopic particles, which is one of the basic properties of microscopic particles. Microscopic particles sometimes exhibit wave behavior (when they are less significant) and sometimes particle behavior (when they are less significant), acting as wave or particle properties under different conditions. The wavelength and frequency of the wave means that it is extended in space and time. A particle can always be observed with a definite position and momentum in space and time. The complementarity principle states that quantum phenomena can be observed by one method or another conjugate method, but not by two mutually conjugate methods at the same time.

3 No-cloning theorem

3.1 Introduction

An important consequence of quantum physics is the no-cloning theorem, which states that it is impossible to construct a system that can copy any quantum bit exactly without interfering with the original one. The linear nature of quantum mechanics is the fundamental reason for this principle.

The principle of non-cloning is the foundation of quantum informatics. Quantum information is transmitted in a channel and cannot be copied by a third party to steal information without causing interference with quantum information. This principle is therefore the cornerstone of quantum cryptography.

3.2 Proof

Fro the proof of the No-cloning theorem, we can use proof by caontradiction(an indirect proof), which is a proof that establish the truth or the validity of a proposition,by showing that assuming the proposition to be false leads to a contraction. * 1. We assume that there is a system that can make a complete copy of any qubit. * 2. ${|{\phi}\rangle}$ and ${|{\psi}\rangle}$ is two arbitary quantum state, We want to cpoy these two state to another state ${|\kappa\rangle}$,which is none businese with the previous qbits. We use a unitary operator $U$ to describe this process. The copy must meet the following properties.

$$U(|\phi\rangle\otimes|\kappa\rangle)=|\phi\rangle\otimes|\phi\rangle$$ $$U(|\psi\rangle\otimes|\kappa\rangle)=|\psi\rangle\otimes|\psi\rangle$$ Do the Dot product between $\langle{U}(\phi\otimes\kappa)|$ and $|U(\psi\otimes\kappa)\rangle$, we will get, $$\langle{U}\phi\otimes\kappa)|U(\psi\otimes\kappa)\rangle= \langle\phi\otimes\phi|\psi\otimes\psi \rangle $$ $$\langle{U}(\phi\otimes\kappa)|U(\psi\otimes\kappa)\rangle= \langle\phi\otimes\kappa|\psi\otimes\kappa\rangle $$ so the following equation will apear: $$\langle\phi\otimes\phi|\psi\otimes\psi\rangle=\langle\phi\otimes\kappa|\psi\otimes\kappa\rangle$$

$$ \langle\phi|\psi\rangle\langle\phi|\psi\rangle = \langle\phi|\psi\rangle\langle\kappa|\kappa\rangle$$

Because of $\langle\kappa|\kappa\rangle = 1$, for the equation hold, $$\langle\phi|\psi\rangle\langle\phi|\psi\rangle=\langle\phi|\psi\rangle$$

The condition that should be met is $$\langle\phi|\psi\rangle=0$$ or

$$\langle\phi|\psi\rangle=1$$ That means，$\phi$ and $\psi$ are orthogonal or equanl. It does not satisfy that $\phi$ and $\psi$ are arbitary quantum state.
